Wait... The NFL Honors Awards are Rigged?¶

While there are a ton of jokes and memes out there saying the NFL is rigged, it is important to not take jokes seriously. Obviously, the NFL is not rigged. Just imagine how challenging it would be for the NFL to influence the outcome of every play, of every game.

Despite this, is it possible that the NFL is pulling some strings? When it comes to awards, I would say definitely. While it's impossible to change the trajectory of every pass, tackle, touchdown, or field goal, it is very easy to influence the outcome of which players get the most recognition. At the end of the day, when there is no way to objectively say who's the best, a big corporation, like the NFL, is going to pick whoever will get them the most clicks on the internet for an award. My visualizations all tell this story by showing how vast outliers are often ignored. I dont think there is any way this story could do harm, especially with the 2021 season being well over a year over. However, I have learned to never underestimate the anger and rage of sports fans. Then again, I am just using data to voice an opinion of one of my favorite sports leagues.

Now, let's take a look at the recipients of the annual MVP award.

Notice how every single MVP (Most Valuable Player) of the last eight years is a QB (Quarterback)? What is even more bizarre about this list is that the second most common MVP winning position is the running back (RB). The running back in the modern NFL is a combination of two positions, the half back (HB) and full back (FB). When we split up the running back position and redistribute evenly, the second most common MVP winning position is the full back with only ~10 MVP winners. There are around 22 unique positions in the NFL, but only ONE seems to be able to win the MVP regularly, the quarterback.

Why is this?

Well for starters, the quarterback is by far the most recognizable position on the gridiron. If you ask a non-American to name football positions, chances are, the first thing they will say is the quarterback. The quarterback leads the offense. Quarterbacks are typically the only players on the field that can throw the ball. Perhaps this leadership role is what makes quarterbacks recognizable. However, quarterbacks being recognizable does not mean they are the most valuable or 'best' player on the field.

Usually, it's very hard to say someone is the best objectively, unless they are so far ahead of others in their field. This most recent year of the NFL had many quarterbacks that had a realistic shot at being deemed the most valuable QB. Quarterbacks are judged by wins, losses, completions, completion percentage, touchdowns, interceptions, and more. The most accurate way to judge a quarterback is by QBR (Quarterback Rating), a matrix designed to combine all of the previously stated stats into one number from zero to 158.3. In 2021, Aaron Rodgers won MVP. On paper this seems fitting, but in reality, we are only comparing Rodgers against the 31 other starting NFL quarterbacks.

Since every NFL position is different, comparing one to another is difficult. Instead, we should look at how much better the best of a given position is when stacked up against others in the given position.

One of the biggest highlights of the 2021 season was Cooper Kupp. Kupp is a wide-receiver for the Los Angeles Rams. Not only did Kupp lead the league in receiving yards, but also receptions, and touchdowns.

In each one of these categories, Kupp dismantled the competition. Breaking records left and right. Kupp became the first receiver since 2005 to win the triple crown (leading in receptions, yards, and touchdowns). On top of that Kupp would compete against 95 other starting wide receivers, three times as many as what Rodgers competes against. Surely the NFL would recognize this and award Kupp the MVP right?

Wrong. Instead, the NFL is stubborn and refuses to give the MVP to any player other than quarterbacks. Even with the best statistical season ever for a wide receiver, Kupp is relegated to Offensive Player of the Year, an award that has become second place to the elusive MVP.

Defensive stats are often extremely hard to quantify, as the defense is reacting to whatever the offense is doing. However, sometimes there are impressive outliers. In the 2021 season, TJ Watt tied the all-time NFL record for most sacks in a single season. What is even more impressive about this is that Watt did this by playing 2 games less than his closest competition. Watt was awarded defensive player of the year, but tying an all-time record is far more impressive than beating 31 quarterbacks in QBR over one season.

Kupp and Watt's record-breaking seasons are anomalies, they happen once in a decade. Not only did Kupp and Watt beat the best of 2021, but they beat the thousands that have played before them. Still, the NFL would rather put their most recognizable quarterbacks on a higher pedestal than record breaking position player.

Conclusion¶

Why does the NFL make questionable decisions when it comes to awards? Well, seeing how the NFL has exploded in popularity over the last few decades, I think it is fair to say the NFL knows how to manipulate its viewers. By distributing questionable awards, the NFL gets more people talking. This leads to more attention. The NFL does not care if their viewers are arguing as the league is still getting more attention. Generally, arguments are a huge buzz on social media, why not manufacture them?

In the end, Im not saying you shouldnt watch the NFL or other sports leagues. However, I think fans should learn to not worry about who wins the awards at the end of the season. Even though we have a massive amount of statistics at our disposal, the NFL Honors Awards are quite subjective when we listen to the experts.

I was surprised to see that Pro Football Reference had an option to convert their statistics to a .csv or excel file. Transitioning this information to graphs was a little frustrating at first, but got much easier as I got into a rhythm. Due to most of these stats being player oriented, I used bar graphs often to sort this data. For the position distribution, I used a catplot, which I had no idea even existed until creating this project.

Unfortunately, I had some failed attempts in putting this story together. After completing my rough draft, I experimented with various types of plots. Scatter plots and line plots really didnt do much in visualizing my story. I also tried using the .add function found on Seaborns website, but I couldnt figure out how to work that. Instead, I looked for a work around.

Some of the most fun I had with this assignment was the customization of my visualizations. The palette feature at first seemed very limited, until I created my own palette. For each bar graph, I researched the hex values of each players teams colors. Even though the other methods of visualization might not be the best for this data, it would still be fun to know what all of them do.
